Social Implications Grade 10

Social implications looks at how ICTs affect people's health, the environment, society and legal/ethical choices. This topic integrates with all other theory areas.

Ergonomics and Health

Ergonomics is the science of designing workspaces and equipment to fit the user safely and comfortably, reducing the risk of injury.

Correct Workstation Setup

  • Chair height: feet flat on the floor, knees at 90°
  • Monitor: at eye level, arm's length away, no glare
  • Keyboard: elbows at 90°, wrists straight (not bent)
  • Sit up straight — back supported by the chair
  • Take regular breaks — every 30–60 minutes, look away and stretch

Green Computing

Green computing refers to using computers in an environmentally responsible way — reducing energy use and electronic waste (e-waste).

Green Computing Practices

  • Switch off monitors and computers when not in use
  • Use power-saving / sleep mode settings
  • Buy energy-efficient devices (Energy Star rating)
  • Print only when necessary (save paper)
  • Recycle old devices responsibly — do NOT throw in general waste
  • Donate still-working equipment to schools or charities
E-WASTE

E-waste (electronic waste) contains toxic substances like lead, mercury and cadmium. Improper disposal can contaminate soil and water. Always take old devices to registered recycling points.

Economic Reasons for Using Computers

Computers save money and improve productivity in many ways:

  • Save paper — digital documents replace printed ones
  • Save labour costs — automated tasks replace manual work
  • Save communication costs — email and VoIP are cheaper than phone calls and postage
  • Efficiency — tasks are completed faster and with fewer errors
  • Accuracy — calculations and data entry are more reliable
  • Reliability — consistent output 24/7 without fatigue

Digital Citizenship

Digital citizenship = being a responsible, ethical and safe user of technology and the Internet. It involves:

  • Behaving online as you would in person — respectfully and honestly
  • Protecting your own and others' personal information
  • Using technology for legitimate, legal purposes only
  • Respecting copyright and intellectual property
  • Reporting cyberbullying and online threats

Digital Footprint

DEFINITION

A digital footprint is the trail of data you leave behind whenever you use the Internet — searches, social media posts, online purchases, websites visited. This data can be tracked, stored and used by companies and others. It is almost impossible to completely erase your digital footprint.

  • Active footprint: information you deliberately share (posts, profiles, sign-ups)
  • Passive footprint: information collected without your direct knowledge (cookies, browsing history, location data)

The POPIA Act (Protection of Personal Information Act)

POPIA is a South African law that protects citizens' personal information. Key points:

  • Organisations must have a lawful reason to collect your personal data
  • Data must be stored securely and not shared without permission
  • You have the right to know what data is held about you
  • You can request that incorrect data be corrected or deleted

Personal information includes: name, ID number, email address, phone number, location data, health information, financial details.

Computer Crime (Types)

Information theft
Stealing private data (passwords, company secrets) without authorisation
Data theft
Copying, deleting or altering someone else's data
Identity theft
Stealing someone's personal information to impersonate them (e.g. for financial fraud)
EXAM TIP — Social Implications Questions

Exam questions often provide a scenario and ask you to identify the social issue, suggest a solution, or evaluate the impact. Always read the context carefully and link your answer to specific terms: ergonomics, e-waste, POPIA, digital footprint, netiquette, copyright, piracy, etc.
