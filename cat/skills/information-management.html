Information Management (PAT) All Grades

The PAT (Practical Assessment Task) is a year-long research project worth a big chunk of your CAT mark. Information management is the skill of finding, evaluating, processing and presenting information to answer a question — and it is also tested in theory.

WHAT IS INFORMATION MANAGEMENT?

Turning raw data into useful information: deciding what you need to know, gathering it from reliable sources, organising and analysing it (often in a spreadsheet), and communicating your findings clearly and honestly.

The Information Management Process

1. Task Definition

Work out exactly what you must find out before you start searching.

  • Write a clear task definition — one sentence stating what the project must answer.
  • Break it into focus questions (3–5 smaller questions that, answered together, solve the task).
  • Decide what information you need and what format the final product takes (report, spreadsheet, presentation, web page).
Good vs weak focus questions

Weak: "Cellphones?" (too vague). Good: "How does daily screen time affect Grade 11 learners' sleep?" — specific, measurable, answerable.

2. Finding Sources

Primary sources
Information you gather first-hand — surveys, questionnaires, interviews, observations, your own measurements
Secondary sources
Information gathered by others — websites, books, articles, reports, videos

Smart searching

  • Use specific keywords, not whole sentences.
  • Put exact phrases in "quotation marks" to search them as a unit.
  • Use site: to search one site (e.g. load shedding site:gov.za) and filetype:pdf for documents.
  • Use -word to exclude a term (e.g. jaguar -car).

Gathering your own data (questionnaires)

  • Ask clear, unbiased questions — don't lead the respondent to an answer.
  • Use closed questions (Yes/No, ratings, multiple choice) so answers are easy to analyse in a spreadsheet.
  • Ask a large enough, fair sample of people for results to mean something.
  • Tools: Google Forms or Microsoft Forms export straight to a spreadsheet.

3. Evaluating Sources

Not everything online is true. Judge every secondary source before you trust it:

CheckAsk yourself…
AuthorityWho wrote it? Are they an expert/organisation, or anonymous?
AccuracyIs it backed by evidence? Does it match other reliable sources?
CurrencyWhen was it published/updated? Is it still current?
Objectivity / BiasIs it trying to inform, or to sell/persuade? Is one side pushed?
CoverageIs it detailed and relevant to your focus questions?
Bias & fake news

A source can be accurate but biased (one-sided). Prefer .gov, .edu, .ac.za and well-known organisations. Cross-check a fact in at least two independent sources before you use it.

4. Processing the Data

Turn your raw data into something meaningful — usually in a spreadsheet:

  • Capture survey results into Excel; clean out errors and blanks.
  • Use functions (SUM, AVERAGE, COUNTIF, %) to summarise the data.
  • Create charts — a pie chart for proportions, a bar/column chart for comparisons, a line chart for trends.
  • Make sure every chart has a title and labels so it can be understood on its own.
Data → Information

"73, 81, 64, 90…" is data. "The class average is 77% and 80% passed" is information — it answers a question. The PAT is graded on the information you produce, not the raw numbers.

5. Drawing Conclusions

  • Answer each focus question using your processed data — refer to your charts/figures.
  • Then answer the overall task definition.
  • Make recommendations where appropriate — what should be done based on your findings?
  • Only draw conclusions your evidence supports — don't invent or exaggerate.

6. Presenting the Findings

  • Choose the right format: a word-processing report, a presentation, or a web page.
  • Structure: title page → table of contents → introduction → findings (with charts) → conclusions & recommendations → bibliography.
  • Use consistent, professional formatting: headings/styles, page numbers, captions on figures.
  • Write in your own words — summarise, don't copy-paste.

Referencing, Bibliography & Plagiarism

Bibliography
A list at the end of all sources you used (author, title, date, URL, date accessed)
In-text reference
A note in the body showing where a specific fact/quote came from
Plagiarism
Presenting someone else's work (including AI output) as your own — a serious offence that can fail your PAT
Avoid plagiarism

Always acknowledge your sources. Put quotes in quotation marks, summarise in your own words, and list every source in the bibliography. Copying from a website (or pasting AI-generated text) without crediting it is plagiarism.

PAT TIP — save your evidence

Keep every draft, source and screenshot in well-named folders (see File & Data Management) and back up regularly. The PAT is built up in phases — losing your work or your sources mid-year is costly and hard to redo.
