Networks & WAN (T2) Grade 12

Grade 12 network coverage focuses on Wide Area Networks (WANs), Internet services, connection types and ISP considerations including throttling, CAPs and informed buying decisions.

Term 2

Wide Area Networks (WAN)

DEFINITION

A WAN (Wide Area Network) connects networks across large geographical areas — cities, countries, even globally. The Internet is the largest WAN in the world, connecting billions of devices across every continent.

LANWAN
CoverageBuilding/campusCities, countries, global
OwnershipOwned by one organisationUses leased infrastructure (ISP, telecom)
SpeedFast (1 Gbps+)Varies (ISP bandwidth)
ExampleSchool networkInternet; company linking branches nationally
Term 2

Intranet & Private Networks

Intranet
A company's private network, available only to connected users inside the organisation (like a private version of the internet)
Extranet
An intranet that gives limited access to selected outsiders, e.g. suppliers or clients
VPN
Virtual Private Network — a secure, encrypted connection giving safe remote access to a private network
EXAM TIP — reasons NOT to use an intranet

A popular question asks why a company might NOT use an intranet — e.g. expensive to set up and maintain, requires skilled staff, and a small business may simply not need one.

Term 2

Internet Services

Instant messaging
Real-time text chat (WhatsApp, Telegram, Teams); advantage: immediate; disadvantage: always-on pressure, distraction
VoIP
Voice calls over Internet (Skype, WhatsApp calls); advantage: free/cheap; disadvantage: needs internet, quality varies
File sharing
Share files via cloud (Google Drive, OneDrive) or P2P; advantage: easy to collaborate; disadvantage: copyright issues with P2P
Grid computing
Combines computing power of many computers over the internet to solve one complex problem (scientific research)
Cloud computing
Access software, storage and processing over the Internet (SaaS, IaaS, PaaS)
Government services
Online ID/passport renewal, NSFAS applications, traffic fine payments, e-Citizen portals
Streaming
Receiving and playing media continuously in real time (Netflix, YouTube, Spotify) — no download needed
Downloading
Saving a complete copy of media/file to your device before using it

Term 2

ISP — Internet Service Provider

An ISP provides Internet access to homes and businesses. They lease bandwidth from telecommunications companies.

ISP vs Infrastructure Provider

Don't confuse them: an ISP (e.g. Afrihost, Webafrica, MWEB) sells you internet access. An infrastructure provider (e.g. Vuma, Openserve) owns and maintains the physical fibre/cable network but does not sell directly to you.

Key ISP Terms

Bandwidth
The maximum amount of data that can be transmitted per second (like the width of a pipe)
Broadband
High-speed Internet connection (always on); generally 25 Mbps or faster
CAP (Data Cap)
The maximum amount of data allowed per month before being throttled or charged extra (e.g. 50 GB/month)
Bundle
A package deal — often data + voice minutes + SMS for one price
Throttling
ISP deliberately slows your connection speed after you exceed your data cap or during peak times
Traffic Shaping
ISP prioritises certain types of traffic (e.g. slows streaming but keeps browsing fast) to manage network congestion
Fair Use Policy (FUP)
Rules about "uncapped" plans — excessive use may still be throttled; "uncapped" does not mean unlimited at full speed

Making Informed Buying Decisions — Internet

Factors to Consider

FactorQuestions to ask
Speed (Mbps)How many users? What will it be used for? (Video streaming needs 25 Mbps+; gaming needs low latency)
Data cap (CAP)How much data do I use per month? Will uncapped be better value?
Coverage / SignalIs fibre available in my area? What mobile signal strength do I get?
CostMonthly fee, installation cost, router cost, contract period
ReliabilityDoes the ISP have good uptime? Is fibre more reliable than wireless?
ContractMonth-to-month or fixed term? Penalties for early cancellation?
Router/modemProvided by ISP or must you buy? Does it support Wi-Fi 5/6?

Wireless Network Considerations

  • Access points: more access points = better Wi-Fi coverage in large buildings
  • Coverage area: walls and interference reduce Wi-Fi range; 5 GHz is faster but shorter range; 2.4 GHz travels further but is slower
  • Mbps (megabits per second): measure of data transmission speed — note: 1 MB/s = 8 Mbps
EXAM TIP — Throttling vs Traffic Shaping

Throttling: slows ALL your Internet speed after you reach your data cap. Traffic shaping: selectively slows specific traffic types (e.g. slows YouTube but keeps WhatsApp fast) — used during peak hours to manage congestion. Both are used by ISPs to manage network load.

BITS vs BYTES — a classic catch

Connection speed is in bits per second (Mbps), but file size is in bytes (MB). Since 8 bits = 1 byte, a 80 Mbps line downloads at most about 10 MB per second (80 ÷ 8). Watch the small "b" vs big "B".

Common Exam Mistakes

Bandwidth and a data cap mean the same thing.
Bandwidth = speed (how fast); a cap = the amount of data allowed per month.
An ISP and an infrastructure provider are the same.
An ISP sells you access; an infrastructure provider (e.g. Vuma) owns the fibre and doesn't sell to you directly.
A LAN can cover a whole country.
A LAN covers one building/site; a WAN covers a large area (the internet is a WAN).
