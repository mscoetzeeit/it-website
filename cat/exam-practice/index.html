How CAT is examined

CAT has two papers. Paper 1 (Practical) is done on a computer — you open data files and complete tasks in Word, Excel, Access and an HTML editor. Paper 2 (Theory) is written — multiple choice, matching, true/false, and scenario-based questions about hardware, software, networks, the internet and social implications.

The two papers at a glance

 Paper 1 — PracticalPaper 2 — Theory
Done onA computer (live application files)Paper & pen (written)
CoversWord, Excel, Access*, HTML, file managementSystems tech, networks, internet, info management, social implications, solution development

Marks forThe correct result in the fileCorrect facts, the right number of facts, and applying them to the scenario

*Databases (Access) are examined practically from Grade 11. Grade 10 practical focuses on Word, Excel and HTML.

Before you start — read this first

🎯 Exam Technique & Question Verbs

What "name", "state", "explain", "discuss" and "motivate" actually want from you — and how mark allocations tell you how much to write. The single biggest source of lost marks in CAT.

How to use this section

  1. Cover the memo. Answer on paper (theory) or on a computer (practical) before you peek.
  2. Watch the marks. A (2) means two separate, correct facts. One fact = one mark, no matter how long the sentence.
  3. Mark honestly. Each ✓ in the memo is one mark. Give yourself the tick only if your point matches.
  4. Re-read the question's verb. If it said "explain" and you only "named", you would lose marks in the real exam.
  5. Keep a list of every question you got wrong and revise that topic page before trying again.
